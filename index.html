High School

My journey to become an engineer began in high school when I joined FIRST Robotics Competition(FRC) team DERT 2040. Through FRC, I was taught the design process, how to brainstorm and debate ideas, fabrication, and leadership. The first Saturday of January marks the beginning of the competition season. Once the challenge (2019 challenge) has been given, we have six short weeks to design and fabricate a 150-pound competition-ready robot. Through the four years I was a member of DERT, I held three leadership positions. I was the lab manager for a year, responsible for the safe use of the machine shop and training team members in safe practices. I held the position of mechanical lead for two years, where I led the design and fabrication of the robot from the CAD model to welding to final assembly. As mechanical lead, I lead round table discussions on design, progress, and strategy. I held the position of team leader, responsible for facilitating communication between the five sub-teams and overseeing time and task management throughout the season. Throughout my four years on DERT I learned many skills that I use today, such as time and task management, conflict resolution, and the ability to fabricate a prototype quickly.

Through the connections I made during my time at DERT, I secured a summer internship at Caterpillar during my Junior year. Since this was a high school position, there wasn’t much for me to do except shadow other employees. Throughout the summer, I worked in the mechatronics digital engine test room, assisted with engine control module debugging, tabulated GPS data, ran engine model test configurations, and worked on a new diagnostic device for data collection.

College

Fall 2019 I attended Bradley University, a college that was only a few miles from where I lived. I chose to pursue a bachelor’s degree in mechanical engineering. In my first semseter, I was asked to coach a FIRST Lego League(FLL) team. FLL is a competition for 4th through 8th graders to be exposed to the design process, teamwork, and the idea of coopertition. I stayed at Bradley for two years before transferring to Iowa State University for the Fall 2021 semester. My reason for transferring was that the mechanical engineering I had been exposed to wasn’t very interesting to me. Bradley mostly had us working with HVAC concepts, or conventional engines. At Iowa State, I could study aerospace engineering. While the basic principals are similar to those in mechanical engineering, there are much more interesting applications in air and space vehicals/mechanisms. At Iowa State, I helped found the Autonomous Robotics Club (ARC). ARC operates with the goal of giving students the opportunity to learn autonomous coding skills such as vision processing, control algorithms, and path planning. At ARC, we use software such as Robot Operating System (ROS), OpenCV, Gazebo, PyTorch, and many other code bases. I will graduate in December 2023.
